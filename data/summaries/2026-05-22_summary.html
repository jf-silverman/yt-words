Summary: Pay for quality and stick with discipline; best-of-breed stocks beat emotion-driven picks despite premium valuations.

  • Best-of-breed (highest-quality, well-managed) companies deserve premium P/E multiples — P&G, Nvidia, and Apple are gold standards worth the price premium.
  • Nvidia is Cramer's best pick ever; always looked expensive near-term but had exceptional out-year earnings growth. Apple at $23 in 2016 was a fabulous call despite skeptics predicting decline.
  • Own best-of-breed stocks long-term, don't trade them; high-quality companies reward patience when temporarily weak. If you believe in the business, hold tight through volatility.
  • Forward and out-year P/E multiples matter more than trailing P/E; Nvidia looks expensive now but has excellent growth ahead — this justifies the premium.
  • Macro rule: Watch bonds constantly; rising long-term interest rates make the entire stock market worth less and especially hurt growth stocks.
  • Micro rule: When a CEO or CFO resigns for 'undisclosed personal reasons,' sell immediately — almost always signals something is wrong at the company.
  • Corrections are like rain: inevitable and predictable. Keep 10-20% cash ready on sidelines to buy quality stocks into weakness.
  • Use the Market Edge Oscillator to time market extremes: +5 = overbought (trim positions), −5 = oversold (buy), −10 = major bottom (buy heavily).
  • Hope is not an investing strategy; never buy garbage low-quality penny stocks hoping they bounce back. The market renders harsh but accurate judgments.
  • You must be able to explain every stock pick to another person in clear terms; if you can't articulate the story, you don't understand it well enough to own it.
  • Know how the company makes money and how earnings are supposed to grow; 90% of meme stock buyers have no idea what they own — terrible foundation for investing.
  • Beware the Wall Street promotion machine; SPAC deals in 2020-2021 used impossible 10-year projections that would land founders in prison if submitted to a real IPO process.
  • Never buy SPACs; money managers on TV often are 'talking their book,' trying to bail out of positions when they tout beaten-down stocks.
