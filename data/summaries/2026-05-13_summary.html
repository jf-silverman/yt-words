▶ Episode Summary: The Bedrock Rules of Investing [0:17]

Master the big-picture discipline: know yourself, do homework, stay flexible, and exploit market irrationality.

  • Define your investment objectives clearly before buying any stock—retirement, college savings, and discretionary portfolios require different risk profiles.
  • Start with a minimum $10K in a low-cost S&P 500 index fund; it is the most important bedrock of any portfolio.
  • Build a diversified portfolio of 5–10 individual stocks across distinct sectors only after doing thorough homework on each company.
  • Develop a thesis for each holding; be able to explain the investment story to another person and recognize when that thesis breaks down.
  • Stay flexible: when the facts change or your thesis is no longer valid, be willing to sell immediately—avoid falling in love with any stock.
  • The woulda-coulda-shoulda game is destructive; remove tickers from your watch lists after selling to avoid obsessing over missed gains or losses.

▶ Episode Summary: The Market Makes Mistakes—Exploit Them [34:30]

Don't assume market action is rational; spot mispriced stocks and sector-wide selloffs driven by ETF mechanics.

  • Stock prices do not always reflect underlying fundamentals; they are also a product of perception and market mechanics.
  • The rise of ETFs, especially FANG-focused ones, creates 'ETF-ization' where unrelated stocks get dragged down together by sector flows.
  • When the worst operator in an industry reports bad numbers, the whole group can sell off unfairly—this creates buying opportunities for quality names.
  • Example: Tenable reported bad cybersecurity numbers in April 2023; the entire group sold off, creating a great entry point for Palo Alto Networks (PANW).
  • Sometimes the market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent; prioritize making money over being philosophically right.
  • Your goal is not necessarily to be right; it's to make money by recognizing and exploiting market mistakes.
  • Don't presume stocks deserve to go down just because they do; 'deserves got nothing to do with it' (Clint Eastwood in Unforgiven).
