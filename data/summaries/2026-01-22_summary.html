▶ Closing Commentary: Earnings Season Psychology [40:00]

Psychology matters most in earnings season: expect misses, catch CEOs with credibility and catalyst; stocks too-hated (Procter, Boeing) or too-loved (GE Aerospace) reverse sharply.

  • Three case studies: Procter & Gamble (weak quarter, rallied 3%), GE Aerospace (beat big, dropped 7.4%), Boeing (no report, held strong).
  • Procter strategy: bought into weakness at $146, $141 because (1) expectations already crushed, (2) new CEO with credibility, (3) long history of execution, (4) solid dividend support.
  • Procter Q4: weak revenue and organic sales, but management telegraphed miss in December; psychological expectations so low that any stability looked good; CEO clarity on turnaround in China, Latin America catalyzed rally.
  • Boeing thesis: bought relentlessly down (50%, 60%, 70% declines) on management's promise that cash flow would exceed expectations; ignore noise and follow the cash flow metric.
  • GE Aerospace trap: best-of-breed CEO (Larry Culp), stock ran from $62 to $318 in two years, reported fantastic numbers (beat on every line, margin improvement remarkable), yet stock dropped 7.4%.
  • GE Aerospace lesson: A-student getting more A's doesn't get praise if traders expect A+; stock too-loved going into quarter; momentum players disappointed by lack of upside surprise.
  • Key insight: stocks down on weak prints can rally (Procter), stocks up on strong prints can sell (GE Aerospace); psychology of expectations-vs.-delivery drives price more than actual numbers.
  • Contrarian setup: identify stocks too-hated (down 24% like Procter) or too-loved (at all-time high like GE); buy the hated ones ahead of quarter if CEO credible and catalyst real.
  • Cramer's closing: 'When a stock gets too hated or too-loved, it's often due for trajectory change; earnings provide the catalyst.' Psychology beats fundamentals in short term.
