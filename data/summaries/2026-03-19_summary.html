▶ Closing Commentary: Buying Quality Stocks in Free Fall [39:03]

Conviction buying on pullbacks works; Dell case study shows how to build positions pyramid-style as best companies fall on sentiment, not fundamentals.

  • Charitable trust gets into great stocks at low prices by buying when they're in free fall and no one wants them. Requires discipline and conviction.
  • Buying stocks in tailspin is psychologically hard; you often feel like a chump when they drop further. Easy to panic-sell at a loss instead of averaging down. But that's a mistake.
  • Dell Technologies example: fell from $168 (October peak) to $110 as AI sector rotated, despite strong fundamentals — company buying back stock heavily, servers becoming principal way businesses buy Nvidia chips, vendor financing advantage vs. competitors (HP Enterprise, Supermicro lack clean balance sheets for lending).
  • Last month Dell raised buyback by $10B, boosted dividend 20%, reported high backlog and best book of business in industry. Semiconductor price fears (DRAM squeeze) did not materialize; Michael Dell was ready.
  • Key strategy: buy 'pyramid style' — buy small initially, then buy more (bigger size) as stock gets cheaper, improving cost basis. You never catch bottom but you get better average entry.
  • Requisite: must do homework to confirm nothing fundamentally wrong during decline. If company has earnings, good balance sheet, strong CEO (like Michael Dell), conviction buying on weakness can work spectacularly.
  • Method does NOT work for companies with no earnings, miserable balance sheets, or 'magical thinking' stocks — those may never bounce.
  • Most investors miss these moves because they wait for 'coast to clear,' but coast never clears in real-time. Must act when widely hated.
